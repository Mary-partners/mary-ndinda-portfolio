01 — My Story

Welcome to the Life of a Persistent Polymath

This is not a resume. It's the story of how I learned to think across boundaries, build under pressure, and find the pattern that holds everything together.

Where It Started

I didn't grow up with a clear path. There was no family tradition in finance, no inherited network in innovation, no roadmap to the work I do today. What I had was curiosity that wouldn't sit still, and a stubbornness that refused to accept that you had to pick one thing and stay there forever.

Growing up in Nairobi, I watched systems everywhere — how markets moved, how people survived shocks, how small businesses built resilience with almost nothing. I noticed early that the people who thrived weren't specialists. They were the ones who could see connections between things that looked unrelated. The shopkeeper who understood weather patterns. The trader who understood community trust. The mother who ran household finances with the discipline of a CFO.

That became my education before any formal one began.

I didn't choose to be cross-disciplinary. Complexity chose me. I just learned to stop fighting it.

The Finance Years

My formal career began in finance. Numbers made sense to me — not in the abstract way they teach in textbooks, but as stories. Every financial statement told me something about decisions, trade-offs, and risk appetite. I could see the choices behind the numbers.

I earned my Bachelor of Commerce in Finance, then a Master of Science in Commerce. I became a CPA finalist. I worked across financial consulting, treasury management, and business partnering. I learned how capital moves, where it gets stuck, and why the gap between what organisations plan and what they execute is almost always a financial architecture problem.

But the more I understood finance, the more I realised it wasn't enough on its own. Finance without operational context is just spreadsheets. Finance without systems thinking is just reporting. Finance without understanding people is just compliance.

I needed more tools.

Into the Operating Room

I moved into operational roles — not because I was bored with finance, but because I was convinced that the real leverage was at the intersection of strategy and execution. I wanted to be in the room where decisions became reality.

I helped build cross-border payment corridors between Africa and Asia. I worked with central bank licensing in Kenya and Uganda. I managed treasury across currencies, partners, and regulatory environments that were changing by the week. We scaled remittance volumes to $100M. I learned that operational excellence in emerging markets is not a luxury, it is survival.

At Xprizo, I led finance and country operations across Kenya, navigating digital payments regulation and the Central Bank of Kenya licensing process. Every day was a lesson in translating regulatory complexity into business execution.

I started to see myself not as a finance person who happened to operate, but as an operator who happened to speak finance fluently.

The Climate Realisation

Then climate changed everything.

Not in the abstract, distant way that climate is often discussed in boardrooms. In the immediate, financial, operational way. I started working with agricultural lenders whose portfolios were being reshaped by weather volatility. I saw how a delayed rainy season wasn't just an environmental event — it was a credit event. A liquidity event. A survival event for thousands of borrowers.

At Atram Inc, I built climate early-warning systems that delivered financial signals to over 5,000 SME clients. I designed anticipatory liquidity facilities — financial infrastructure that could respond to climate shocks before they became defaults. I worked with Fortune Credit to protect a portfolio of 10,000 borrowers, 65% of them women in agriculture, by structuring mechanisms that kept capital flowing when the weather wouldn't cooperate.

I contributed to Kenya's National Disaster Risk Financing Strategy for 2026–2030, working with the National Treasury and IIED. This wasn't academic. This was building the financial architecture that a country would use to absorb shocks.

The insight that changed my trajectory: Climate risk is not an environmental problem that sometimes affects finance. It is a financial problem that is permanently reshaping how capital, insurance, and credit work in the most vulnerable economies on earth.

Building, Not Just Advising

I've coached over a thousand entrepreneurs through venture-building and investment readiness pathways. I've worked with GiveDirectly, the Mastercard Foundation, the UK Government's Manufacturing Africa programme, and the UN Environment Programme. I've managed innovation portfolios spanning seven countries across East, West, and Southern Africa, and into Latin America.

But I'm not an advisor who watches from the side. I build. I've structured enterprise acceleration systems that served 35,000 borrowers. I've designed replication methodologies and scaling playbooks that turned one-country pilots into multi-country programmes. I'm now expanding climate risk product scaling to Colombia, working with a microfinance institution that serves 1.6 million customers.

At the Africa Management Institute, I lead solutions design for innovation scaling and enterprise acceleration — turning catalytic capital into sustainable, revenue-generating systems.

And I'm pursuing a Doctorate in Business Administration, because the questions I'm working on deserve the rigour of deep research alongside the urgency of execution.

The Persistent Polymath

I call myself a persistent polymath because that's the truest description of how I work. I'm persistent because the problems I care about — climate resilience, financial inclusion, institutional capacity — don't yield to short bursts of attention. They require sustained commitment across years and setbacks.

I'm a polymath because I refuse to be confined to one discipline when the problems I'm solving span several. Finance alone can't solve climate risk. Technology alone can't create institutional resilience. Policy alone can't build investable pipelines. You need someone who can hold all of these in their head at once and build the connective tissue between them.

That's what I do.

I'm not interested in being the smartest person in one room. I'm interested in being the person who can walk into any room and find the pattern that connects what everyone else is working on.

What I'm Looking For

I'm not looking for the next job. I'm looking for the next problem worth solving. I'm drawn to complexity, to the places where systems break down or haven't been built yet. I want to work with people who understand that the most valuable thing in the room isn't the answer — it's the ability to hold the right question long enough for a real solution to emerge.

If that resonates — if you're building something that requires this kind of thinking — I'd like to hear about it.
