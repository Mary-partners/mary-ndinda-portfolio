04 — Foresight

What I See Coming

These are the patterns I'm watching. Not predictions — structural shifts that are already underway and will reshape how we work, build, and allocate capital.

01

Climate risk is becoming a financial event

We've spent decades treating climate as an environmental issue. That era is ending. Climate risk is now a credit risk, a liquidity risk, an insurance pricing problem, and a sovereign fiscal constraint — all at once.

The institutions that will survive this transition are the ones building financial infrastructure that can respond to climate signals in real time. Not ESG reports filed quarterly. Not sustainability pledges. Actual financial plumbing — early-warning triggers connected to liquidity mechanisms, insurance products that price weather volatility accurately, and credit models that account for agricultural exposure.

I've seen this firsthand. When you design an anticipatory liquidity facility for an agricultural lender in Kenya, you're not doing "climate work." You're doing financial engineering under uncertainty. The distinction matters because it determines whether institutions take it seriously or treat it as a compliance exercise.

The gap right now is enormous. Most financial institutions in emerging markets have no climate risk infrastructure at all. The ones that build it first will have a structural advantage that compounds over decades.

02

Founders are outgrowing their systems

Across Africa and other emerging markets, there's a generation of founders who are capable, ambitious, and deeply knowledgeable about their markets. What they lack isn't vision or hustle. It's systems.

They've outgrown the informal processes that got them started. They need financial models that hold up under investor scrutiny. Governance structures that scale. Operational discipline that survives the first year after funding. And most importantly, they need these things translated into their actual context — not imported wholesale from Silicon Valley playbooks.

The investor readiness gap isn't about pitch decks. It's about institutional maturity. The founders who close that gap fastest will capture a disproportionate share of the capital flowing into emerging markets.

I see this as one of the highest-leverage problems to work on right now. Not by providing more advice, but by building the systems and tools that help founders level up their operational infrastructure.

03

Systems are becoming more valuable than advice

The consulting and advisory industry is built on a model where expertise is delivered through people. You pay for access to someone's judgment, frameworks, and relationships. This model works, but it doesn't scale, and it creates dependency rather than capability.

What I see emerging is a shift toward systems that codify institutional knowledge into repeatable, improvable processes. Instead of hiring an advisor to tell you what to do, you deploy a system that helps your team make better decisions consistently.

This isn't about replacing human judgment with automation. It's about building the infrastructure that makes human judgment more effective. Decision architectures. Risk frameworks. Operational playbooks. Financial models that update with real data rather than sitting in a drawer.

The advisory firms and operators who make this transition — from selling expertise to building systems — will be far more valuable than those who don't.

04

Cross-disciplinary operators are becoming essential

The most important problems in the world right now — climate adaptation, financial inclusion, institutional resilience, technology governance — don't fit into single disciplines. They require people who can hold finance, technology, policy, and operations in their head simultaneously.

The traditional career path trains you to go deep in one domain. That's valuable, but it's not sufficient for the problems we're facing. What's needed are operators who can translate between domains, see the interactions between systems, and build solutions that work at the intersections.

I've been building this kind of career — deliberately, persistently, across finance, climate, operations, and innovation — because I believe this is what the next decade requires. Not more specialists working in silos. More polymaths who can build the bridges between them.

Organisations that recognise this and create space for cross-disciplinary operators will have a significant strategic advantage. Those that don't will keep solving the same problems in the same ways, wondering why they're not making progress.

05

Africa's opportunity is readiness, not just capital

There's a persistent narrative that what Africa needs is more capital. More investment. More funding. And while capital is important, it's not the binding constraint I see most often.

The binding constraint is readiness. Institutional readiness to absorb and deploy capital effectively. Operational readiness to scale programmes across geographies. Financial readiness to meet investor expectations without losing mission integrity. Governance readiness to manage growing complexity.

The organisations and economies that invest in building this readiness infrastructure — the systems, processes, and capabilities that make capital deployment effective — will be the ones that capture the most value from the increasing flow of investment into African markets.

This is not a criticism. It's an opportunity. And it's one that requires a very specific kind of work: building institutional capacity at speed, across sectors, with limited resources and enormous ambition. That's exactly the kind of work I'm built for.
