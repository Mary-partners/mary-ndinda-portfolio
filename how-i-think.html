02 — Frameworks

This Is How I Think

I don't start with a discipline. I start with the problem. Then I pull from whatever domain gives me the clearest path to a working solution.

The Operating Principle

Most people are trained to think within a single domain. Accountants think like accountants. Technologists think like technologists. Policy people think like policy people. This works until you encounter a problem that spans all three.

I've built my career on the conviction that the most important problems in finance, climate, and development are fundamentally cross-disciplinary. They sit at the intersections where one discipline's solution creates another discipline's constraint. You can't design a climate risk facility without understanding credit operations. You can't build an innovation scaling framework without understanding revenue models. You can't structure a disaster risk financing strategy without understanding both sovereign fiscal space and community-level resilience.

My approach is to hold multiple frameworks simultaneously and look for the configuration that makes them work together, not against each other.

The best solution is usually the one that satisfies constraints from three domains at once. If it only works in one, it's not a solution — it's a wish.

Systems Over Solutions

I don't build one-off fixes. I build systems that can absorb variation, scale across contexts, and improve over time. A good system makes the next decision easier, not harder. This is why I focus on operational architecture — the underlying structure that determines whether an organisation can execute consistently or only episodically.

Translation as Core Skill

I translate between domains. I take a regulatory requirement and make it operational. I take an impact thesis and make it financially viable. I take a climate signal and make it a credit decision. Most institutional failure isn't a knowledge problem — it's a translation problem. People have the information but can't convert it across contexts.

Execution Under Ambiguity

I'm comfortable operating without a complete map. Emerging markets, climate uncertainty, and early-stage ventures all share a common trait: the information you need to make a perfect decision doesn't exist yet. I've trained myself to make structured, reversible decisions with incomplete data — and to build feedback loops that correct course quickly.

Financial Architecture as Strategy

Finance isn't a back-office function. It's the operating system. How you structure capital, incentives, and risk allocation determines what an organisation can and cannot do. I design financial architectures that enable strategy, not just report on it. This means treasury design, blended finance structuring, revenue modelling, and capital deployment frameworks.

How This Shows Up in Practice

In Climate Risk

I don't treat climate as an ESG checkbox. I treat it as a financial variable that changes how lending, insurance, and capital allocation work. When I designed anticipatory liquidity facilities for agricultural lenders, I was building financial infrastructure that responds to weather data in real time — not reporting on it after the damage is done.

In Innovation Scaling

I don't scale programmes by replicating activities. I scale by codifying the underlying logic — the decision frameworks, governance structures, and incentive mechanisms — into replicable systems. This is why a pilot in Kenya can become a platform in Senegal, Nigeria, and Colombia without losing its effectiveness.

In Enterprise Acceleration

I don't coach entrepreneurs with motivation. I coach them with structure. Investment readiness isn't about pitch decks. It's about financial models that hold up under scrutiny, governance systems that investors trust, and operational discipline that survives the first year after funding.

In Institutional Design

I see organisations as systems, not org charts. When I join a team — whether as a CFO, an operations lead, or a strategy consultant — I look for the structural gaps between what the organisation intends and what it can actually execute. Then I build the connective tissue: processes, controls, information flows, and decision architectures that close the gap.

The through-line in all of my work: I take high-level strategic intent and convert it into implementable operational reality. Policy to execution. Theory to system. Ambition to infrastructure.

What I Look for in Problems

I'm drawn to a specific kind of problem:

  • It spans more than one discipline
  • The stakes are real — people's livelihoods, institutional survival, or systemic resilience
  • There's no off-the-shelf solution
  • Execution is as hard as strategy
  • It requires someone who can hold the technical, financial, and human dimensions at the same time

If you're working on something that fits this description, I'd be interested in understanding it better.
