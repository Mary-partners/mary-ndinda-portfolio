01 — My Story

Welcome to the Life of a
Persistent Polymath

This is not a resume. It's the story of how I learned to think across boundaries, build under pressure, and find the pattern that holds everything together.

Where It Started

I didn't grow up with a clear path. There was no family tradition in finance, no inherited network in innovation, no roadmap to the work I do today. What I had was curiosity that wouldn't sit still, and a stubbornness that refused to accept that you had to pick one thing and stay there forever.

Growing up in Nairobi, I watched systems everywhere — how markets moved, how people survived shocks, how small businesses built resilience with almost nothing. I noticed early that the people who thrived weren't specialists. They were the ones who could see connections between things that looked unrelated.

The shopkeeper who understood weather patterns. The trader who understood community trust. The mother who ran household finances with the discipline of a CFO.

That became my education before any formal one began.

I didn't choose to be cross-disciplinary. Complexity chose me. I just learned to stop fighting it.

The Finance Years

My formal career began in finance. Numbers made sense to me — not in the abstract way they teach in textbooks, but as stories. Every financial statement told me something about decisions, trade-offs, and risk appetite. I could see the choices behind the numbers.

I earned my Bachelor of Commerce in Finance, then a Master of Science in Commerce. I became a CPA finalist. I worked across financial consulting, treasury management, and business partnering. I learned how capital moves, where it gets stuck, and why the gap between what organisations plan and what they execute is almost always a financial architecture problem.

But the more I understood finance, the more I realised it wasn't enough on its own. Finance without operational context is just spreadsheets. Finance without systems thinking is just reporting. Finance without understanding people is just compliance.

I needed more tools.

Into the Operating Room

I moved into operational roles — not because I was bored with finance, but because I was convinced that the real leverage was at the intersection of strategy and execution. I wanted to be in the room where decisions became reality.

At Wapi Pay, I helped build cross-border payment corridors between Africa and Asia. I worked with central bank licensing in Kenya and Uganda. I managed treasury across currencies, partners, and regulatory environments that were changing by the week. We scaled remittance volumes to a million dollars a month.

I learned that operational excellence in emerging markets isn't a luxury — it's survival.

At Xprizo, I led finance and country operations across Kenya, navigating digital payments regulation and the Central Bank of Kenya licensing process. Every day was a lesson in translating regulatory complexity into business execution.

I started to see myself not as a finance person who happened to operate, but as an operator who happened to speak finance fluently.

The Climate Realisation

Then climate changed everything.

Not in the abstract, distant way that climate is often discussed in boardrooms. In the immediate, financial, operational way. I started working with agricultural lenders whose portfolios were being reshaped by weather volatility. A delayed rainy season wasn't just an environmental event — it was a credit event. A liquidity event. A survival event for thousands of borrowers.

At Atram Inc, I built climate early-warning systems that delivered financial signals to over 5,000 SME clients. I designed anticipatory liquidity facilities — financial infrastructure that could respond to climate shocks before they became defaults.

I worked with Fortune Credit to protect a portfolio of 10,000 borrowers, 65% of them women in agriculture, by structuring mechanisms that kept capital flowing when the weather wouldn't cooperate.

I contributed to Kenya's National Disaster Risk Financing Strategy for 2026–2030, working with the National Treasury and IIED.

The insight that changed my trajectory: Climate risk is not an environmental problem that sometimes affects finance. It is a financial problem that is permanently reshaping how capital, insurance, and credit work in the most vulnerable economies on earth.

Building, Not Just Advising

I've coached over a thousand entrepreneurs through venture-building and investment readiness pathways. I've worked with GiveDirectly, the Mastercard Foundation, the UK Government's Manufacturing Africa programme, and the UN Environment Programme.

I've managed innovation portfolios spanning seven countries across East, West, and Southern Africa, and into Latin America.

But I'm not an advisor who watches from the side. I build. I've structured enterprise acceleration systems that served 35,000 borrowers. I've designed replication methodologies and scaling playbooks that turned one-country pilots into multi-country programmes.

I'm now expanding climate risk product scaling to Colombia, working with a microfinance institution that serves 1.6 million customers.

At the Africa Management Institute, I lead solutions design for innovation scaling and enterprise acceleration — turning catalytic capital into sustainable, revenue-generating systems.

And I'm pursuing a Doctorate in Business Administration, because the questions I'm working on deserve the rigour of deep research alongside the urgency of execution.

The Persistent Polymath

I call myself a persistent polymath because that's the truest description of how I work. I'm persistent because the problems I care about — climate resilience, financial inclusion, institutional capacity — don't yield to short bursts of attention. They require sustained commitment across years and setbacks.

I'm a polymath because I refuse to be confined to one discipline when the problems I'm solving span several. Finance alone can't solve climate risk. Technology alone can't create institutional resilience. Policy alone can't build investable pipelines.

You need someone who can hold all of these in their head at once and build the connective tissue between them. That's what I do.

I'm not interested in being the smartest person in one room. I'm interested in being the person who can walk into any room and find the pattern that connects what everyone else is working on.

What I'm Looking For

I'm not looking for the next job. I'm looking for the next problem worth solving. I'm drawn to complexity, to the places where systems break down or haven't been built yet.

I want to work with people who understand that the most valuable thing in the room isn't the answer — it's the ability to hold the right question long enough for a real solution to emerge.

02 — Frameworks

This Is How
I Think

I don't start with a discipline. I start with the problem. Then I pull from whatever domain gives me the clearest path to a working solution.

The Operating Principle

Most people are trained to think within a single domain. Accountants think like accountants. Technologists think like technologists. This works until you encounter a problem that spans all three.

I've built my career on the conviction that the most important problems are fundamentally cross-disciplinary. They sit at the intersections where one discipline's solution creates another discipline's constraint.

The best solution is usually the one that satisfies constraints from three domains at once. If it only works in one, it's not a solution — it's a wish.
01

Systems Over Solutions

I don't build one-off fixes. I build systems that can absorb variation, scale across contexts, and improve over time. A good system makes the next decision easier, not harder.

This is why I focus on operational architecture — the underlying structure that determines whether an organisation can execute consistently or only episodically.

02

Translation as Core Skill

I translate between domains. I take a regulatory requirement and make it operational. I take an impact thesis and make it financially viable. I take a climate signal and make it a credit decision.

Most institutional failure isn't a knowledge problem — it's a translation problem. People have the information but can't convert it across contexts.

03

Execution Under Ambiguity

I'm comfortable operating without a complete map. Emerging markets, climate uncertainty, and early-stage ventures all share a common trait: the information you need to make a perfect decision doesn't exist yet.

I've trained myself to make structured, reversible decisions with incomplete data — and to build feedback loops that correct course quickly.

04

Financial Architecture as Strategy

Finance isn't a back-office function. It's the operating system. How you structure capital, incentives, and risk allocation determines what an organisation can and cannot do.

I design financial architectures that enable strategy, not just report on it. Treasury design, blended finance, revenue modelling, and capital deployment frameworks.

How This Shows Up in Practice

I don't treat climate as an ESG checkbox. I treat it as a financial variable that changes how lending, insurance, and capital allocation work. When I designed anticipatory liquidity facilities for agricultural lenders, I was building financial infrastructure that responds to weather data in real time — not reporting on it after the damage is done.

I don't scale programmes by replicating activities. I scale by codifying the underlying logic — the decision frameworks, governance structures, and incentive mechanisms — into replicable systems. This is why a pilot in Kenya can become a platform in Senegal, Nigeria, and Colombia without losing its effectiveness.

I don't coach entrepreneurs with motivation. I coach them with structure. Investment readiness isn't about pitch decks. It's about financial models that hold up under scrutiny, governance systems that investors trust, and operational discipline that survives the first year after funding.

I see organisations as systems, not org charts. When I join a team I look for the structural gaps between what the organisation intends and what it can actually execute. Then I build the connective tissue: processes, controls, information flows, and decision architectures that close the gap.

The through-line in all of my work: I take high-level strategic intent and convert it into implementable operational reality. Policy to execution. Theory to system. Ambition to infrastructure.

04 — Foresight

What I See
Coming

These are the patterns I'm watching. Not predictions — structural shifts already underway that will reshape how we work, build, and allocate capital.

01

Climate risk is becoming a financial event

We've spent decades treating climate as an environmental issue. That era is ending. Climate risk is now a credit risk, a liquidity risk, an insurance pricing problem, and a sovereign fiscal constraint — all at once.

The institutions that will survive this transition are the ones building financial infrastructure that can respond to climate signals in real time. Not ESG reports filed quarterly. Actual financial plumbing — early-warning triggers connected to liquidity mechanisms, insurance products that price weather volatility accurately.

The gap right now is enormous. Most financial institutions in emerging markets have no climate risk infrastructure at all. The ones that build it first will have a structural advantage that compounds over decades.

02

Founders are outgrowing their systems

Across Africa and other emerging markets, there's a generation of founders who are capable, ambitious, and deeply knowledgeable about their markets. What they lack isn't vision or hustle. It's systems.

They've outgrown the informal processes that got them started. They need financial models that hold up under investor scrutiny. Governance structures that scale. Operational discipline that survives the first year after funding.

The investor readiness gap isn't about pitch decks. It's about institutional maturity. The founders who close that gap fastest will capture a disproportionate share of the capital flowing into emerging markets.

03

Systems are becoming more valuable than advice

The consulting industry is built on a model where expertise is delivered through people. This model works, but it doesn't scale, and it creates dependency rather than capability.

What I see emerging is a shift toward systems that codify institutional knowledge into repeatable, improvable processes. Instead of hiring an advisor to tell you what to do, you deploy a system that helps your team make better decisions consistently.

The advisory firms and operators who make this transition — from selling expertise to building systems — will be far more valuable than those who don't.

04

Cross-disciplinary operators are becoming essential

The most important problems right now — climate adaptation, financial inclusion, institutional resilience — don't fit into single disciplines. They require people who can hold finance, technology, policy, and operations in their head simultaneously.

Organisations that recognise this and create space for cross-disciplinary operators will have a significant strategic advantage. Those that don't will keep solving the same problems in the same ways.

05

Africa's opportunity is readiness, not just capital

There's a persistent narrative that what Africa needs is more capital. While capital is important, it's not the binding constraint. The binding constraint is readiness.

Institutional readiness to absorb and deploy capital effectively. Operational readiness to scale programmes across geographies. Financial readiness to meet investor expectations without losing mission integrity.

The organisations and economies that invest in building this readiness infrastructure will capture the most value from the increasing flow of investment into African markets. That's exactly the kind of work I'm built for.
